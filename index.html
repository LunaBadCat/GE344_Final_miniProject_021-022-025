Conflagration

Conflagration is defined as large-scale fires that often result in significant damage to human life, animal life, health, and property. They can arise either as accidental occurrences or be intentionally ignited. In the case of a particularly immense fire, a firestorm may develop, characterized by a central column of rising heated air that generates strong inward winds, thus providing oxygen to fuel the fire. Conflagrations can lead to casualties, encompassing fatalities or injuries caused by burns, structural collapses, attempts to escape, and inhalation of smoke.

Causes and Types

During a conflagration, there is a significant movement of air and combustion products. The hot gaseous products of combustion tend to rise, creating an influx of denser, colder air into the combustion zone. In some cases, this influx of air is so intense that it leads to the development of a firestorm. Within a building, the intensity of gas exchange is influenced by factors such as the size and positioning of openings in walls and floors, the height of the ceiling, and the quantity and characteristics of the combustible materials present. Wildfires, on the other hand, are fires that take place in forests or other undeveloped areas and can escalate into a conflagration. An urban conflagration is characterized as a large and destructive fire that extends beyond natural or man-made barriers. It is expected to result in substantial financial losses and may or may not involve fatalities. In an urban conflagration, the fire extends beyond a single city block and causes widespread destruction across entire sections of a city.
